About

Sankranthi is a very special and joyful harvest festival celebrated mainly in India, especially in Andhra Pradesh, Telangana, Tamil Nadu, Karnataka, and Maharashtra. It usually falls in January and marks the beginning of the Sun’s northward journey (Uttarayanam), which is considered very auspicious. 1. Harvest Festival Sankranthi celebrates the harvesting of crops. Farmers thank the Sun God, earth, rain, and cattle for a good yield. It represents prosperity and hard work. 2. Four Days of Celebration Bhogi: Old items are burned in a bonfire, symbolizing the removal of negativity and welcoming new things. Sankranthi (Makara Sankranthi): Main festival day; people worship the Sun God and prepare special dishes. Kanuma: Dedicated to cattle; farmers decorate and worship cows and bulls. Mukkanuma (in some regions): Celebrated with village gatherings and traditional games. 3. Traditional Food Special sweets like tilgul (sesame and jaggery), pongal, ariselu, and sakinalu are prepared. These foods symbolize sweetness, unity, and warmth. 4. Kite Flying Kite flying is one of the most exciting parts of Sankranthi. The colorful sky represents happiness, freedom, and celebration. 5. Rangoli and Decorations Homes are decorated with colorful rangoli (muggulu) made with rice flour, flowers, and lamps to welcome prosperity. 6. Cultural and Family Bonding Sankranthi brings families together. People wear new clothes, visit relatives, share sweets, and enjoy cultural activities. 7. Spiritual Importance The festival signifies new beginnings, positivity, and gratitude toward nature and the Sun God.
